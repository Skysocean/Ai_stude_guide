Chapter 1 — Introduction to Artificial Intelligence+
What is Artificial Intelligence?
According to the father of AI, John McCarthy: "The science and engineering of making intelligent machines, especially intelligent computer programs."
AI is a way of making a computer, computer-controlled robot, or software think intelligently in the same manner intelligent humans think.
AI is concerned with the design of intelligence in an artificial device. The term was coined by John McCarthy in 1956.
Intelligence = the ability to acquire, understand, and apply knowledge to achieve goals in the world.
AI is the study of mental faculties through the use of computational models.
AI is the study of intellectual/mental processes as computational processes.
An AI program will demonstrate a high level of intelligence to a degree that equals or exceeds the intelligence required of a human in performing some task.
AI is unique — it shares borders with Mathematics, Computer Science, Philosophy, Psychology, Biology, Cognitive Science and many others.
Although no clear definition exists for AI or Intelligence, it can be described as: an attempt to build machines that like humans can think and act, able to learn, and use knowledge to solve problems on their own.

4 Approaches In Detail
Cognitive Science (Think Human-Like):
a. Requires a model for human cognition precise enough to allow simulation by computers.
b. Focus is not just on behavior and I/O — looks at the reasoning process itself.
c. Goal is not just to produce human-like behavior but to produce a sequence of steps similar to those followed by a human solving the same task.
Laws of Thought (Think Rationally):
a. Study of mental faculties through computational models; study of computations that make it possible to perceive, reason, and act.
b. Focus on inference mechanisms that are probably correct and guarantee an optimal solution.
c. Goal is to formalize the reasoning process as a system of logical rules and procedures of inference.
d. Develop systems of representation for inferences like: "Socrates is a man. All men are mortal. Therefore Socrates is mortal."
Turing Test (Act Human-Like):
a. The art of creating machines that perform functions requiring intelligence when performed by people; how to make computers do things at which people are currently better.
b. Focus is on action, not intelligent behavior centered around world representation.
c. Example — Turing Test: 3 rooms contain a person, a computer, and an interrogator. Interrogator communicates by teletype (to avoid machine imitating voice/appearance). Machine aims to fool interrogator — if it succeeds, machine is intelligent.
Rational Agent (Act Rationally):
Agent always performs the right action — the action that causes the agent to be most successful in the given percept sequence. Characterized by PEAS: Performance Measure, Environment, Actuators, Sensors.

What is Intelligence Composed of? — 5 Components
Intelligence is intangible. It is composed of: Reasoning, Learning, Problem Solving, Perception, Linguistic Intelligence.
1. Reasoning
The set of processes that enables us to provide basis for judgement, making decisions, and prediction. Two broad types:
Inductive ReasoningDeductive Reasoning
Conducts specific observations to make broad general statementsStarts with a general statement and examines possibilities to reach a specific, logical conclusion
Even if all premises are true, the conclusion can be falseIf something is true of a class of things in general, it is also true for all members of that class
Example: "All women over 60 are grandmothers. Shalini is 65 years. Therefore, Shalini is a grandmother."Example: "Nita is a teacher. All teachers are studious. Therefore, Nita is studious."
2. Learning
The activity of gaining knowledge or skill by studying, practising, being taught, or experiencing something. Enhances awareness of subjects of study. Possessed by humans, some animals, and AI-enabled systems.
8 Types of Learning:
Auditory Learning — learning by listening and hearing. E.g., students listening to recorded audio lectures.
Episodic Learning — learn by remembering sequences of events one has witnessed or experienced. This is linear and orderly.
Motor Learning — learning by precise movement of muscles. E.g., picking objects, writing.
Observational Learning — learn by watching and imitating others. E.g., a child mimicking her parent.
Perceptual Learning — learning to recognize stimuli seen before. E.g., identifying and classifying objects and situations.
Relational Learning — learning to differentiate among stimuli on basis of relational properties rather than absolute properties. E.g., adding 'little less' salt when cooking potatoes that were too salty last time.
Spatial Learning — learning through visual stimuli such as images, colors, maps. E.g., a person creating a roadmap in mind before following the road.
Stimulus-Response Learning — learning to perform a particular behavior when a certain stimulus is present. E.g., a dog raising its ear on hearing a doorbell.
3. Problem Solving
The process in which one perceives and tries to arrive at a desired solution from a present situation by taking some path, which is blocked by known or unknown hurdles.
Also includes decision making — the process of selecting the best suitable alternative out of multiple alternatives to reach the desired goal.
4. Perception
The process of acquiring, interpreting, selecting, and organizing sensory information. Presumes sensing. In humans, aided by sensory organs. In AI, perception mechanism puts data acquired by sensors together in a meaningful manner.
5. Linguistic Intelligence
One's ability to use, comprehend, speak, and write the verbal and written language. Important in interpersonal communication.
Difference Between Human and Machine Intelligence
Humans perceive by patterns; machines perceive by set of rules and data.
Humans store and recall information by patterns; machines by searching algorithms. E.g., the number 40404040 is easy to remember because its pattern is simple.
Humans can figure out the complete object even if some part is missing or distorted; machines cannot correctly do this.
Scientific Goal vs Engineering Goal
Scientific Goal: To determine which ideas about knowledge representation, learning, rule systems, search, etc., explain various sorts of real intelligence. (Psychologists, philosophers, cognitive scientists)
Engineering Goal: To solve real world problems using AI techniques such as knowledge representation, learning, rule systems, search. (Computer scientists and engineers)

Why AI Works More in Expert Task Domain — VERY IMPORTANT
Humans learn mundane tasks since birth (perception, speaking, language, locomotion). Then Formal Tasks, then Expert Tasks.
For humans, mundane tasks are easiest to learn. This was considered true for machines too — early AI work was concentrated in mundane task domain.
Later, it turned out machines require more knowledge, complex knowledge representation, and complicated algorithms for mundane tasks (because they require common sense which is hard to encode).
This is why AI work is now more prospering in the Expert Task domain — expert tasks need expert knowledge without common sense, which is easier to represent and handle (IF-THEN rules).
Chapter 4 — Agents and Environments+
Core Concepts
An AI system is composed of an agent and its environment. The agents act in their environment. The environment may contain other agents.
Agent — anything that can perceive its environment through sensors and acts upon that environment through effectors.
Human agent: sensory organs (eyes, ears, nose, tongue, skin) = sensors; hands, legs, mouth = effectors.
Robotic agent: cameras and infrared range finders = sensors; motors and actuators = effectors.
Software agent: encoded bit strings = its programs and actions.
Agents Terminology — All Key Terms
Performance Measure of Agent — the criteria which determines how successful an agent is.
Behavior of Agent — the action that agent performs after any given sequence of percepts.
Percept — the agent's perceptual inputs at a given instance (the current input).
Percept Sequence — the history of all that an agent has perceived till date.
Agent Function — a map from the percept sequence to an action.
PEAS — Performance Measure, Environment, Actuators, Sensors — characterizes the problem the agent solves.
Rationality
Rationality = the status of being reasonable, sensible, and having good sense of judgment.
Rationality is concerned with expected actions and results depending upon what the agent has perceived. Performing actions with the aim of obtaining useful information is an important part of rationality.
Ideal Rational Agent: capable of doing expected actions to maximize its performance measure, based on: (1) its percept sequence, (2) its built-in knowledge base.
Rationality of an agent depends on: (1) performance measures (degree of success), (2) agent's percept sequence till now, (3) agent's prior knowledge about the environment, (4) the actions the agent can carry out.
A rational agent always performs the right action — the action that causes the agent to be most successful in the given percept sequence.
Structure of Intelligent Agents
Agent = Architecture + Agent Program
Architecture = the machinery that an agent executes on.
Agent Program = an implementation of an agent function.
4 Types of Agents — Full Detail
1. Simple Reflex Agents
Choose actions only based on the current percept.
Rational only if a correct decision is made on the basis of current percept alone.
Their environment is completely observable.
Use Condition-Action Rule — a rule that maps a state (condition) to an action. The rule: "What is the world like now?" → "What actions I need to do?"
2. Model-Based Reflex Agents
Use a model of the world to choose their actions. Maintain an internal state.
Model: knowledge about "how the things happen in the world."
Internal State: a representation of unobserved aspects of current state depending on percept history.
Updating state requires: (1) how the world evolves, (2) how the agent's actions affect the world.
3. Goal-Based Agents
Choose their actions in order to achieve goals.
More flexible than reflex agent — knowledge supporting a decision is explicitly modeled, allowing for modifications.
Goal: the description of desirable situations.
Asks: "What is the world like now?" → "What happens if I do action A?" → "What actions I need to do?" → uses Goals to decide.
4. Utility-Based Agents
Choose actions based on a preference (utility) for each state.
Goals are inadequate when: (1) there are conflicting goals only some of which can be achieved, (2) goals have some uncertainty of being achieved and one needs to weigh likelihood of success against importance of a goal.
Asks: "How happy am I by doing action A?" → uses Utility function to decide.
Nature of Environments
Some programs operate in artificial environments confined to keyboard input, databases, file systems, and character output on screen.
Some software agents (softbots) exist in rich, unlimited domains with very detailed, complex environments requiring choice from a long array of actions in real time.
The most famous artificial environment is the Turing Test environment — very challenging as it is highly difficult for a software agent to perform as well as a human.
Turing Test — Full Description
Two persons and a machine to be evaluated participate. One person plays the role of the tester (interrogator). Each sits in a different room.
Tester is unaware of who is machine and who is human. Interrogates by typing and sending questions to both; receives typed responses.
This test aims at fooling the tester. If the tester fails to determine machine's response from the human response, then the machine is said to be intelligent.

Chapter 5 — Search Algorithms+
Introduction to Problem Solving
Problem solving is a process of generating solutions from observed data.
A problem is characterized by: (1) a set of goals, (2) a set of objects, (3) a set of operations. These could be ill-defined and may evolve during problem solving.
Searching is the universal technique of problem solving in AI.
Single-player games (tile games, Sudoku, crossword) use search algorithms to find a particular position.
Steps to Build a Problem-Solving System
1. Define the problem precisely
2. Analyze the problem
3. Isolate and represent the task knowledge necessary to solve the problem
4. Choose the best problem-solving technique and apply it to the particular problem
State Space Representation
The state space representation forms the basis of most AI methods.
State — defined by the specification of the values of all attributes of interest in the world.
Operator — changes one state into the other; has a precondition (value of attributes before application) and a set of effects (attributes altered by operator).
Initial State — where you start. Goal State — partial description of the solution.
Formal Description of a Problem: (1) Define state space with all possible configurations; (2) Specify initial state(s); (3) Specify goal states; (4) Specify rules (operations) available.
State-Space Problem Formulation — 4 Items
A problem is defined by four items:
1. Initial state e.g., "at Arad"
2. Actions or successor function: S(x) = set of action-state pairs. e.g., S(Arad) = {Arad → Zerind, ...}
3. Goal test (or set of goal states): e.g., x = "at Bucharest", Checkmate(x)
4. Path cost (additive): e.g., sum of distances, number of actions. c(x,a,y) is step cost, assumed ≥ 0.
A solution = a sequence of actions leading from the initial state to a goal state.

4 Properties of Search Algorithms
1. Completeness — Is a solution guaranteed to be found if at least one solution exists?
2. Optimality — Is the solution found guaranteed to be the best (lowest cost) solution if more than one exists?
3. Time Complexity — Upper bound on time required to find a solution as a function of problem complexity.
4. Space Complexity — Upper bound on storage space (memory) required at any point during the search.
Single Agent Pathfinding Problems
Games like 3×3 eight-tile, 4×4 fifteen-tile, 5×5 twenty-four tile puzzles are single-agent-path-finding challenges.
They consist of a matrix of tiles with a blank tile. Player arranges tiles by sliding horizontally/vertically into blank space to accomplish some objective.
Other examples: Travelling Salesman Problem, Rubik's Cube, Theorem Proving.
Brute-Force Search Strategies
Most simple — do not need domain-specific knowledge. Work fine with small number of possible states.
Requirements: state description, set of valid operators, initial state, goal state description.
Breadth-First Search (BFS)
Starts from root node, explores neighboring nodes first, then moves to next level neighbors. Generates one tree at a time until solution found.
Implemented using FIFO queue. Provides shortest path to solution.
If branching factor = b and depth = d, nodes at level d = b^d. Total nodes created (worst case) = b + b² + b³ + ... + b^d.
Disadvantage: Each level of nodes saved for creating next one → consumes enormous memory. Space requirement is exponential.
Can check duplicate nodes. Complexity depends on number of nodes.
Depth-First Search (DFS)
Implemented in recursion with LIFO stack. Creates same set of nodes as BFS, only in different order.
Nodes on single path stored from root to leaf → space requirement is linear. With branching factor b and depth m, storage space = bm.
Disadvantage: May not terminate and go on infinitely on one path. Solution: choose a cut-off depth. If cut-off < d → may fail. If cut-off > d → execution time increases.
Cannot check duplicate nodes. Complexity depends on number of paths.
Bidirectional Search
Searches forward from initial state and backward from goal state until both meet to identify a common state.
Path from initial state concatenated with inverse path from goal state. Each search done only up to half of total path.
Uniform Cost Search
Sorting done in increasing cost of path to a node. Always expands the least cost node.
Identical to BFS if each transition has the same cost. Explores paths in increasing order of cost.
Disadvantage: There can be multiple long paths with cost ≤ C* — Uniform Cost Search must explore them all.
Iterative Deepening Depth-First Search
Performs DFS to level 1, starts over, executes complete DFS to level 2, continues until solution found.
Never creates a node until all lower nodes are generated. Only saves a stack of nodes.
Algorithm ends when it finds solution at depth d. Nodes at depth d = b^d; at depth d-1 = b^(d-1).

Informed (Heuristic) Search Strategies
To solve large problems with large number of possible states, problem-specific knowledge needs to be added to increase efficiency.
Heuristic Evaluation Functions
Calculate the cost of optimal path between two states. For sliding-tile games: count number of moves each tile makes from its goal state and add these for all tiles.
Pure Heuristic Search
Expands nodes in order of heuristic values. Creates two lists: closed list (already expanded) and open list (created but unexpanded).
In each iteration: expand node with minimum heuristic value → create child nodes → place in closed list → apply heuristic to children → place in open list by heuristic value. Shorter paths saved, longer ones disposed.
A* Search
Best-known form of Best First search. Avoids expanding paths that are already expensive, but expands most promising paths first.
f(n) = g(n) + h(n), where:
g(n) = the cost (so far) to reach the node
h(n) = estimated cost to get from the node to the goal
f(n) = estimated total cost of path through n to goal
Implemented using priority queue by increasing f(n).
Greedy Best First Search
Expands the node estimated to be closest to goal. Expands nodes based on f(n) = h(n). Implemented using priority queue.
Disadvantage: Can get stuck in loops. Not optimal.
Local Search Algorithms
Start from a prospective solution and move to a neighboring solution. Can return valid solution even if interrupted at any time before they end.
Hill-Climbing Search
Iterative algorithm. Starts with arbitrary solution and attempts to find better solution by changing a single element incrementally. If change produces better solution, it becomes new solution. Repeat until no further improvements.
Disadvantage: Neither complete nor optimal (can get stuck at local maxima).
Local Beam Search
Holds k number of states at any given time. At start, these states are generated randomly.
Successors of k states computed with objective function. If any successor = maximum value of objective function → stop.
Otherwise: pool all initial k states + k successors (2k states) → sort numerically → select highest k as new initial states → repeat until maximum reached.
Simulated Annealing
Based on process of heating and cooling a metal to change its internal structure. Metal retains newly obtained properties after cooling.
In simulated annealing: temperature is variable. Initially set high → allowed to 'cool' slowly as algorithm proceeds. When temperature is high, algorithm accepts worse solutions with high frequency.
Algorithm: Initialize k=0, L=integer number of variables; From i→j, search performance difference Δ; If Δ≤0 accept, else if exp(-Δ/T(k)) > random(0,1) accept; Repeat for L(k) steps; k=k+1; Repeat until criteria met.
Travelling Salesman Problem
Objective: find low-cost tour that starts from a city, visits all cities exactly once, and ends at the same starting city.
Algorithm: Find all (n-1)! possible solutions → determine minimum cost from each → keep the minimum cost one.
Chapter 6 — Fuzzy Logic+
Introduction
Fuzzy Logic Systems (FLS) produce acceptable but definite output in response to incomplete, ambiguous, distorted, or inaccurate (fuzzy) input.
Fuzzy Logic (FL) is a method of reasoning that resembles human reasoning. It imitates the way of decision making in humans that involves all intermediate possibilities between digital values YES and NO.
The conventional logic block that a computer understands takes precise input and produces definite output as TRUE or FALSE — equivalent to human's YES or NO.
The inventor of fuzzy logic, Lotfi Zadeh, observed that unlike computers, human decision making includes a range of possibilities: CERTAINLY YES → POSSIBLY YES → CANNOT SAY → POSSIBLY NO → CERTAINLY NO.
The fuzzy logic works on the levels of possibilities of input to achieve definite output.
Implementation
Can be implemented in systems with various sizes — from small micro-controllers to large, networked, workstation-based control systems.
Can be implemented in hardware, software, or a combination of both.
Why Fuzzy Logic?
Useful for commercial and practical purposes.
Can control machines and consumer products.
May not give accurate reasoning, but acceptable reasoning.
Helps deal with uncertainty in engineering.
Fuzzy Logic Systems Architecture — 4 Parts (MEMORIZE ORDER)
1. Fuzzification Module: Transforms the system inputs (crisp numbers) into fuzzy sets. Splits input signal into 5 steps:
LabelMeaning
LPx is Large Positive
MPx is Medium Positive
Sx is Small
MNx is Medium Negative
LNx is Large Negative
2. Knowledge Base: Stores IF-THEN rules provided by experts.
3. Inference Engine: Simulates human reasoning process by making fuzzy inference on inputs and IF-THEN rules. Operations used: OR = Max, AND = Min. All results combined to form a final fuzzy result.
4. Defuzzification Module: Transforms the fuzzy set obtained by the inference engine back into a crisp value.
Flow: Crisp Input → Fuzzifier → (Fuzzy Input Set) → Intelligence/Inference Engine ← Rules (Knowledge Base) → (Fuzzy Output Set) → Defuzzifier → Crisp Output
Membership Functions
Allow you to quantify linguistic terms and represent a fuzzy set graphically.
A membership function for a fuzzy set A on the universe of discourse X is defined as: µA: X → [0,1].
Each element of X is mapped to a value between 0 and 1 — called membership value or degree of membership. Quantifies degree of membership of element in X to fuzzy set A.
x-axis = universe of discourse. y-axis = degrees of membership in the [0,1] interval.
Simple membership functions are used — complex functions do not add more precision in output.
Triangular membership function shapes are most common. Others: trapezoidal, singleton, Gaussian.
Development Steps — Air Conditioner Example
Step 1: Define linguistic variables and terms — input/output variables in form of words/sentences. For temperature: very-cold, cold, warm, very-warm, hot. Temperature(t) = {very-cold, cold, warm, very-warm, hot}.
Step 2: Construct membership functions — graph showing degrees of membership across temperature range (0-40°C).
Step 3: Construct knowledge base rules — create room temp vs target temp matrix, then build IF-THEN-ELSE rules:
• IF temperature=(Cold OR Very_Cold) AND target=Warm THEN HEAT
• IF temperature=(Hot OR Very_Hot) AND target=Warm THEN COOL
• IF temperature=Warm AND target=Warm THEN NOCHANGE
Step 4: Fuzzify crisp input data — convert actual temperature reading to fuzzy sets using membership functions.
Step 5: Evaluate rules in inference engine — apply fuzzy operations.
Step 6: Combine results from each rule — using Max (OR) and Min (AND) operations. All results combined to form final fuzzy value.
Step 7: Defuzzify — convert output fuzzy data into non-fuzzy values (crisp output: HEAT / COOL / NO CHANGE).

Chapter 9 — Robotics+
Definitions
Robotics is a domain in artificial intelligence that deals with the study of creating intelligent and efficient robots.
Robots are the artificial agents acting in real world environment.
Objective: Robots are aimed at manipulating the objects by perceiving, picking, moving, modifying the physical properties of objects, destroying it, or to have an effect — thereby freeing manpower from doing repetitive functions without getting bored, distracted, or exhausted.
Robotics is a branch of AI composed of Electrical Engineering + Mechanical Engineering + Computer Science for designing, construction, and application of robots.
Aspects of Robotics
The robots have mechanical construction, form, or shape designed to accomplish a particular task.
They have electrical components which power and control the machinery.
They contain some level of computer program that determines what, when, and how a robot does something.
Difference: Robot System vs Other AI Programs
AI ProgramsRobots

Essential Characteristics of a Robot (5)
Mobility: Possesses some form of mobility.
Programmability: Computational or symbol-manipulative capabilities that a designer can combine as desired (a robot is a computer). Can be programmed to accomplish large variety of tasks. After being programmed, it operates automatically.
Sensors: On or around the device, able to sense the environment and give useful feedback to the device.
Mechanical capability: Enables it to act on its environment rather than merely function as a data processing or computational device (a robot is a machine).
Flexibility: Can operate using a range of programs and manipulates and transports materials in a variety of ways.
Robotics can do: 1. Sensing, 2. Acting, 3. Thinking
Effectors and Sensors
Effectors: Legs, wheels, joints, and grippers. Single purpose: to assert physical forces on the environment.
Sensors: Robots are equipped with sensors. Provide knowledge of real time information on the task environment. Vision sensors compute depth in environment. Tactile sensors imitate mechanical properties of touch receptors of human fingertips.
Robotic Properties (3)
1. They contain enough information for the robot to make the right decisions.
2. They are structured so they can be updated efficiently.
3. They are natural — internal variables correspond to natural state variables in the physical world.
Types of Motion (6)
1. Point-to-point motion: Deliver the robot or its end-effector to a designated target location.
2. Complaint motion: A greater challenge — a robot moves while being in physical contact with an obstacle.
3. Path planning: Find a path from one configuration to another in configuration space. Primary characteristic: involves continuous spaces.
4. Configuration space: First step towards solution to robot motion problem — devise an appropriate problem representation.
5. Cell decomposition: Decomposes the environment into cells.
6. Skeletonization: Reduces environment to a skeleton/graph for pathfinding.
Robot Locomotion — 4 Types
Legged Locomotion
Consumes more power — demonstrates walk, jump, trot, hop, climb up or down.
Requires more number of motors. Suited for rough as well as smooth terrain. Difficult to implement due to stability issues.
Comes in variety of 1, 2, 4, and 6 legs. If multiple legs, leg coordination is necessary.
Formula for gait events: If a robot has k legs, number of possible gait events = N = (2k-1)!
For k=2 legs: N = (2×2-1)! = 3! = 6 events: (1) Lifting Left leg, (2) Releasing Left leg, (3) Lifting Right leg, (4) Releasing Right leg, (5) Lifting both legs, (6) Releasing both legs.
For k=6 legs: N = (2×6-1)! = 11! = 39,916,800 possible events.
Complexity of robots is directly proportional to the number of legs.
Wheeled Locomotion
Requires fewer motors. Easier to implement — fewer stability issues with more wheels. Power efficient compared to legged.
Standard wheel: Rotates around the wheel axle and around the contact.
Castor wheel: Rotates around the wheel axle and the offset steering joint.
Swedish 45° and Swedish 90° wheels: Omni-wheel — rotates around contact point, wheel axle, and the rollers.
Ball or spherical wheel: Omnidirectional wheel — technically difficult to implement.
Slip/Skid Locomotion
Vehicles use tracks as in a tank. Robot steered by moving tracks with different speeds in same or opposite direction.
Offers stability because of large contact area of track and ground.

Computer Vision
Technology of AI with which robots can see. Plays vital role in safety, security, health, access, and entertainment.
Automatically extracts, analyzes, and comprehends useful information from a single image or array of images. Involves development of algorithms for automatic visual comprehension.
Hardware: power supply + image acquisition device (camera) + processor + software + display device for monitoring + accessories (stands, cables, connectors).
Tasks of Computer Vision
OCR (Optical Character Reader): Software to convert scanned documents into editable text.
Face Detection: Reads face and takes picture of that expression. Used to let user access software on correct match.
Object Recognition: Installed in supermarkets, cameras, high-end cars (BMW, GM, Volvo).
Estimating Position: Estimating position of object with respect to camera — e.g., position of tumor in human body.

Inductive vs Deductive reasoning

InductiveSpecific → General. Conclusion CAN be false even if all premises are true.
DeductiveGeneral → Specific. Conclusion MUST be true if premises are true.
Inductive example"All women over 60 are grandmothers. Shalini is 65. Therefore Shalini is a grandmother."
Deductive example"All teachers are studious. Nita is a teacher. Therefore Nita is studious."
